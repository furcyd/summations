Chapter 1 - Turtles all the way down

Here is the point of this book: While it may seem ridiculous and non-sensical to explain something by resorting to an infinity of turtles all the way down, it actually is much more ridiculous and non-sensical to believe that somewhere down there, there's a turtle floating in the air. [...] when you behave in a particular way [...] it is because of the determinism that came just before, which was caused by the determinism just before that, and before that, all the way down. The approach of this book is to show how that determinism works, to explore how the biology over which you had no control, interacting with environment over which you had no control, made you you.
pp. 2-3
We are nothing more or less than the cumulative biological and environmental luck, over which we had no control, that has brought us to any moment.
p. 4
there is no free will. This is what I have concluded, for a long, long time. And even I think that taking that seriously sounds absolutely nutty.
p. 6
no single result or discipline can [disprove free will]. But [...] put all the scientific results together, from all the relevant scientific disciplines, and there's no room for free will. [...] Crucially, all these disciplines collectively negate free will because they are all interlinked, constituting the same ultimate body of knowledge. [...] There is not a single crack of daylight to shoehorn in free will. [...] The first half of the book's point is to rely on this biological framework in rejecting free will. [...] even I think it's crazy to take seriously all the implications of there being no free will. And despite that, the goal of the second half of the book is to do precisely that, both individually and societally. [...] The book's intentionally ambiguous title reflects these two halves [...]
pp. 8-9
[the attitudes held by people writing about free will] come in four basic flavors:
The world is deterministic and there's no free will. I am coming from this perspective of "hard incompatibilism."
The world is deterministic and there is free will. [...] the book will most often be taking on these "compatibilists."
The world is not deterministic; there's no free will. [...] everything important in the world runs on randomness
The world is not deterministic; there is free will. [...] these "libertarian incompatibilists" are a rarity
pp. 10-11
There's a related quartet of views concerning the relationship between free will and moral responsibility. [...] [the latter] calls forth the concept of basic desert, where someone can deserve to be treated in a particular way. [...] these views are:
There's no free will, and thus holding people morally responsible for their actions is wrong. Where I sit.
There's no free will, but it is okay to hold people morally responsible for their actions. This is another type of compatibilism—an absence of free will and moral responsibility coexist without invoking the supernatural.
There is free will, and people should be held responsible. This is probably the most common stance out there.
There is free will, but moral responsibility isn't justified. This is a minority view.
pp. 11-12
my goal isn't to convince you that there is no free will; it will suffice if you merely conclude that there's so much less free will than you thought that you have to change your thinking about some truly important things.
p. 12
If a compatibilist has not wrestled through being challenged by knowledge of the biology of who we are, it's not worth the time trying to counter their free-will belief.
p. 14
What do I mean by free will? [...] Show me a neuron being a causeless cause [of a behavior such as a man pulling the trigger of a gun]
pp. 14-15
What do I mean by determinism? [...] start this topic with [...] "Laplace's demon". [...] Contemporary views of determinism have to incorporate
[1.] the fact that certain types of predictability turn out to be impossible [e.g., due to quantum indeterminacy] [...]
[2.] the role played by meta-level consciousness [e.g., winding up as an adult exactly like one of your parents or their exact opposite] [...]
[3.] [the the fact that,] despite the world being deterministic, things can change.
[...] Imagine a university graduation ceremony. [...] Randomly pick any of the graduates. [...] notice [...] the person who is part of the grounds crew, collecting the garbage from the cans on the perimeter of the event. [...] Trade every factor over which they had no control [e.g., genes , womb, childhood], and you will switch who would be in the graduation robe and who would be hauling garbage cans. This is what I mean by determinism.
